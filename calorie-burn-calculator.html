How to Use the Calorie Burn Calculator

  1. Enter your body weight — heavier individuals burn more calories doing the same activity due to greater mass being moved.
  2. Select your activity — choose from 14 common activities ranging from walking to jump rope.
  3. Enter duration — how many minutes you performed the activity.
  4. Select weight unit — kg or lbs. The calculator converts automatically.
  5. Read calories burned — and compare to all 14 activities at your weight and duration to find the most efficient exercise for your goals.
🔥 Calorie burn reality: Exercise burns fewer calories than most people think and fewer than fitness trackers typically report. A 70kg person burns approximately 300 calories in a 30-minute run — easily replaced by a 300ml glass of fruit juice. Exercise is essential for health and body composition, but weight management primarily happens through diet.

Understanding Exercise and Calorie Burn

📊 MET Values
Metabolic Equivalent of Task — measures exercise intensity relative to rest. Sitting = 1 MET. Walking = 3.5 MET. Running = 9.8 MET. HIIT = 10 MET. Calories burned = MET × weight (kg) × time (hours). This calculator uses published MET values from the Compendium of Physical Activities.
⚡ EPOC Effect
Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption — elevated calorie burning that continues after exercise ends. High-intensity exercise (HIIT, heavy resistance training) produces greater EPOC (lasting hours) than low-intensity exercise. A 30-minute HIIT session may burn 250 calories during exercise but 100–150 additional calories over 24 hours through EPOC.
🏋️ Cardio vs Resistance Training
Cardio burns more calories during the session. Resistance training burns fewer during the session but builds muscle — increasing resting metabolic rate permanently. 5kg of additional muscle burns approximately 50 extra calories per day at rest — accumulating to 18,250 calories per year, or approximately 5 lbs of fat.
📉 Calorie Compensation
After exercise, appetite often increases. Studies show people typically compensate for 30–50% of exercise calories burned through increased food intake. This 'compensation' is why exercise alone rarely produces significant weight loss — dietary change is the more powerful lever.
🎯 Exercise for Health vs Weight
Exercise provides enormous health benefits independent of weight loss: cardiovascular fitness, bone density, muscle mass maintenance, metabolic health, cognitive function, mood regulation. Even without weight loss, regular exercise dramatically reduces risk of heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and depression. These benefits justify exercise regardless of calorie burn.
⌚ Fitness Tracker Accuracy
Most fitness trackers overestimate calorie burn by 27–93% according to Stanford University research. The Fitbit Surge overestimated calories by 52% on average. Use tracker data as a relative measure (comparing days to each other) rather than absolute calorie counts for dietary planning.

Building an Effective Exercise Plan

The minimum effective dose

WHO physical activity guidelines: 150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity or 75–150 minutes of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity per week, plus muscle-strengthening activities 2+ days per week. Meeting these minimums provides the vast majority of health benefits — additional exercise provides diminishing returns for health (though more is generally better for fitness performance). Three 50-minute moderate sessions per week satisfies all guidelines.

Highest calorie-burning activities

For maximum calorie burn per minute: jump rope (12 MET), HIIT (10 MET), running at 10 km/h (9.8 MET), swimming vigorous (9.8 MET), cycling vigorous (10 MET). These high-intensity activities burn 2–3× more calories per minute than walking. However, sustainability matters more than intensity — a daily 30-minute walk is more beneficial long-term than occasional intense sessions followed by days of soreness and avoidance.

Zone 2 training for fat burning

Zone 2 cardio (60–70% of maximum heart rate) — a comfortable pace where you can hold a conversation — burns the highest percentage of calories from fat (as opposed to glycogen). While total calorie burn is lower than high-intensity work, Zone 2 is sustainable daily, improves mitochondrial density and fat-burning capacity over time, and has minimal recovery demands. Endurance athletes and longevity-focused exercisers prioritise Zone 2 for these reasons.

🏃 Exercise hierarchy for body composition: 1) Resistance training 2–3 days/week (preserves and builds muscle). 2) Daily walking 7,000–10,000 steps (high NEAT contribution with minimal recovery cost). 3) 1–2 cardio sessions per week (cardiovascular health). 4) Adequate sleep (7–9 hours — poor sleep increases appetite hormones and reduces fat loss from deficits). This hierarchy outperforms more intense but less consistent approaches.

Frequently Asked Questions

How are calories burned calculated?
This calculator uses MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) values. Calories = MET × weight (kg) × time (hours). MET values are standardised measurements of exercise intensity relative to resting metabolic rate.
Does weight affect calorie burn?
Yes — heavier people burn more calories doing the same activity. A 90kg person burns about 50% more calories than a 60kg person doing the same exercise for the same duration. This is why weight-based calculations are more accurate.
What exercise burns the most calories?
Per hour at 70kg: Jump rope ~840 cal, Running ~700 cal, HIIT ~630 cal, Swimming ~560 cal, Cycling ~525 cal, Walking ~245 cal. High-intensity activities burn more in less time, but lower intensity can be sustained longer.
