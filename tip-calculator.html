🍽️ Tipping etiquette by service type: Sit-down restaurant: 15–20%. Bar: $1–2 per drink or 15–20%. Food delivery: 15–20% (minimum $3–5). Buffet: 10%. Counter service/fast food: optional (10% appreciated). Hotel housekeeping: $2–5 per night.

Understanding Tipping Culture

🌍 Tipping by Country
USA/Canada: Expected 15–20% — servers rely on tips as primary income. UK: 10–15% in restaurants, not expected at pubs. Australia: Not expected, appreciated at 10%. Japan/South Korea: Tipping is considered rude. Europe: 5–10% appreciated, not required.
💰 Why Tip?
In the US, tipped workers are often paid below minimum wage with the expectation that tips make up the difference. Federal tipped minimum wage is $2.13/hour — meaning tips are not optional courtesy but essential income for servers.
📊 Tip on Pre-tax vs Total
Technically correct to tip on pre-tax amount. Practical difference on a $100 bill at 8% tax: tipping on $100 vs $108 = $1.20 difference at 15% tip. Most diners tip on the total for simplicity.
🔢 Quick Mental Math
15% tip: take 10% (move decimal left one), then add half. $64 bill: $6.40 + $3.20 = $9.60 tip. 20% tip: simply take 10% and double. $64 × 2 = $12.80 tip. Rounding up to the nearest dollar is courteous.
💳 Credit Card Tip Timing
When paying by card, the tip is added after the sale is authorised. The server manually enters your written tip amount. Always ensure your copy shows the correct total including tip — errors and fraud occur in both directions.
⚡ Tip Pool vs Individual
Some restaurants pool tips and distribute among all staff including kitchen workers. Others allow servers to keep individual tips. Your tip reaches the people who served you in both systems, just distributed differently.

Tip Calculation for Groups

Splitting bills fairly

For groups with different orders, consider splitting based on what each person ordered rather than equally. Equal splits only work fairly when orders are roughly similar in price. If one person had a $15 meal and another had a $45 meal, equal splitting creates resentment. Many payment apps (Venmo, Splitwise) make item-by-item splitting easy.

The 'round up' culture

Many people round their tip to the nearest dollar for simplicity. On a $47.50 bill with a 20% tip ($9.50), rounding to $10 is a 21% effective tip. Rounding down to $9 is 19%. Rounding up is always appreciated and makes the mental math easier. Some restaurants and apps now pre-fill suggested tip amounts to simplify this decision.

Tipping on takeout

Tipping on takeout is increasingly expected but still optional. If the restaurant prepared your food with care and the order was handled well, 10–15% is appropriate. For large or complex takeout orders, tipping more acknowledges the effort. Digital ordering systems that prompt for tips before you pick up the food have normalised takeout tipping.

🍽️ When service is poor: Rather than leaving no tip (which punishes the server financially), consider leaving 10% and speaking with a manager about the service issue. Many service problems are outside the server's control. If the server was personally responsible for the problem, 10% signals dissatisfaction while still providing some income.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much should I tip at a restaurant?
Standard tipping norms: 10% for adequate service, 15% for good service, 18–20% for excellent service, 25%+ for exceptional service. In the US, 18–20% is now considered standard. In the UK, 10–12.5% is typical.
Should I tip on the pre-tax or post-tax amount?
In the US, it's customary to tip on the pre-tax total. However, many people tip on the full bill for simplicity. The difference is small — on a $100 bill with 10% tax, the tip difference between pre/post-tax is about $1.50 at 15%.
How do I split a bill unequally?
This calculator splits equally by default. For unequal splits, calculate the total (bill + tip) then divide according to what each person ordered. A common approach: each person pays for what they ordered, then split the tip equally.
Is tipping mandatory?
Tipping is legally optional in most countries, but it is a significant cultural expectation in the US, Canada, and some Middle Eastern countries. In Japan and South Korea, tipping is considered rude. In most of Europe and Australia, it's appreciated but not expected.
