About EMI

What is EMI?
Equated Monthly Instalment — the fixed monthly payment for a loan covering both principal repayment and interest.
Formula
EMI = P × r × (1+r)ⁿ / ((1+r)ⁿ − 1) where P = principal, r = monthly rate, n = number of months.
Total interest
Long-tenure loans pay significantly more total interest. Even 1% rate reduction saves substantially over 20 years.

How to Use the EMI Calculator

  1. Enter loan amount — the total amount you want to borrow or the principal of your loan.
  2. Enter interest rate — the annual interest rate offered by your bank or lender. Enter as a percentage (e.g. 8.5 for 8.5% per year).
  3. Set loan tenure — the repayment period in months or years. Longer tenure means lower EMI but higher total interest paid.
  4. Click Calculate — the calculator shows your monthly EMI, total amount payable, and total interest charged over the loan period.
  5. Review the amortisation table — see exactly how much of each monthly payment goes toward principal vs interest. In early months, most of your EMI pays interest.
💡 Key insight: Even a small reduction in interest rate saves significantly over a long loan. On a $200,000 mortgage over 20 years, reducing from 8% to 7% saves over $28,000 in total interest. Always compare rates from multiple lenders before committing.

Understanding EMI

📐 EMI Formula
EMI = P × r × (1+r)^n ÷ ((1+r)^n − 1). Where P = principal, r = monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), n = number of months. This ensures equal payments that fully repay the loan at the end of tenure.
💰 Principal Component
The portion of each EMI that reduces your outstanding loan balance. In early months this is small — most of your payment covers interest. It grows progressively as the loan balance reduces.
📊 Interest Component
The portion covering the cost of borrowing. Highest in month 1 (calculated on full principal), lowest in the final month. Total interest = (EMI × months) − Principal.
📅 Tenure Impact
Doubling tenure roughly halves the EMI but dramatically increases total interest. A $100,000 loan at 8%: 10 years = $1,213 EMI, $45,593 total interest. 20 years = $836 EMI, $100,748 total interest — more than the loan itself.
⚡ Prepayment Benefit
Making extra payments toward principal reduces both tenure and total interest dramatically. Even one extra EMI per year can reduce a 20-year mortgage by 3–4 years.
🏦 Fixed vs Floating Rate
Fixed rate: same EMI throughout. Floating rate: EMI changes with market rates. Fixed gives certainty; floating can be cheaper if rates fall but riskier if rates rise.

Smart EMI Planning

The 40% EMI rule

Financial advisors recommend keeping total monthly EMI payments below 40% of your gross monthly income. This ensures you have sufficient cash flow for living expenses, savings, and emergencies. Before taking any loan, calculate whether the EMI fits within this threshold.

Choosing the right tenure

Shorter tenure means higher EMI but significantly less total interest paid. If you can comfortably afford the higher EMI, choose the shortest tenure possible. The total interest saved over a shorter tenure almost always outweighs the convenience of lower monthly payments.

Prepayment strategy

Most loans allow partial prepayment — paying extra amounts toward principal beyond your regular EMI. Even small additional payments made early in the loan term have an outsized impact on reducing total interest. A $500 extra payment in year 1 of a 20-year mortgage saves several times that amount in interest over the loan's lifetime.

🏦 Before taking any loan: Use this calculator to compare three scenarios — minimum tenure you can afford, maximum tenure offered, and a middle option. The total interest difference between minimum and maximum tenure is often shocking and makes the choice clear.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is EMI?
Equated Monthly Instalment — the fixed monthly payment covering both interest and principal repayment, structured so the loan is fully repaid by end of tenure.
What is the EMI formula?
EMI = P × r × (1+r)ⁿ / ((1+r)ⁿ − 1), where P = principal, r = monthly rate (annual rate / 12 / 100), n = total months.
Shorter vs longer tenure?
Shorter tenure = higher EMI but less total interest. Longer tenure = lower EMI but significantly more interest overall. Choose the shortest tenure your budget can support.
