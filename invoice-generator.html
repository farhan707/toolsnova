Understanding Professional Invoicing

📄 Required Invoice Elements
Business name and address, client name and address, unique invoice number, invoice date, payment due date, itemised list of goods/services, unit prices and quantities, subtotal, tax amount and rate, total due, and payment instructions.
⏰ Payment Terms
Net 15: payment due 15 days from invoice date. Net 30: 30 days (most common for B2B). Net 45/60: used by larger corporations. Early payment discounts: '2/10 Net 30' means 2% discount if paid within 10 days, otherwise full amount due in 30 days.
💳 Payment Methods
Specify accepted payment methods clearly: bank transfer (include account details), PayPal, credit card, check. Multiple options reduce friction and speed payment. Bank transfer is most common for B2B as it has no transaction fees.
📊 Late Payment Fees
Include late payment terms on every invoice: 'Invoices unpaid after 30 days subject to 1.5% monthly interest charge.' This creates a legal basis for charging interest and provides motivation for timely payment. Rarely enforced but effective as a deterrent.
🌍 International Invoicing
For international clients: specify currency clearly, include SWIFT/IBAN for bank transfers, note which party bears transfer fees, be aware of VAT/GST obligations in the client's country for digital services, and consider exchange rate risk for large invoices.
📁 Record Keeping
Keep copies of all invoices for minimum 7 years (tax authority requirement in most countries). Organise by year and client. Reconcile invoices with bank statements monthly. Unpaid invoices beyond 90 days should be escalated or written off as bad debt.

Getting Paid Faster

Invoice timing strategy

Send invoices immediately upon completing work — not at the end of the month. Delay in invoicing is the primary cause of delayed payment. Same-day invoicing while the work is fresh in the client's mind also reduces disputes about scope and pricing. For ongoing work, invoice on a set schedule (1st of each month) so clients budget for your payment.

Follow-up system

Implement a standard follow-up process: day 1 — send invoice with read receipt. Day 7 — friendly reminder if unpaid. Day 21 — firm reminder referencing payment terms. Day 35 — formal notice with late payment fee. Day 60+ — collections or legal action. Most payments are resolved by day 21 — consistent follow-up is more effective than any other strategy.

Protecting yourself legally

A signed contract or purchase order before starting work, combined with a professional invoice referencing that contract, creates a legally enforceable payment obligation. For clients over $5,000, always get a signed contract first. For recurring clients, a standing agreement covers all invoices without individual contracts per project.

💼 Freelancer invoice checklist: Unique invoice number ✓. Your full legal name or business name ✓. Client's full legal name ✓. Detailed description of services ✓. Payment due date ✓. Payment instructions ✓. Late payment terms ✓. Tax registration number if applicable ✓. Each missing element can create payment delays or legal complications.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should be on an invoice?
A professional invoice includes: your business name and contact details, client name and address, unique invoice number, invoice date and payment due date, itemised list of products/services with quantities and prices, subtotal, tax (if applicable), and total amount due.
What is a standard payment term?
NET 30 (payment due within 30 days) is the most common business payment term. NET 15 and NET 60 are also used. Some freelancers use 'due on receipt' or '50% upfront, 50% on delivery'. Always specify payment terms clearly on your invoice.
How do I number invoices?
Use a consistent sequential numbering system: INV-001, INV-002, or INV-2025-001 (year-sequence). Never reuse invoice numbers. Sequential numbering makes it easy to track payments and maintain records for tax purposes.
Should I charge VAT/GST on invoices?
If you're VAT/GST registered, yes — you must charge the applicable tax rate and display it separately on the invoice. If you're below the registration threshold, you generally don't charge or display VAT. Requirements vary by country and business type.
