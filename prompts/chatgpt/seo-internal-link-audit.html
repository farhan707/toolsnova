💡 Pro Tips
Every new page you publish should link to at least 3 existing pages — this is the single most important internal linking habit
Your homepage's link equity is your most valuable asset — be deliberate about which pages it links to directly
Contextual links within body content pass more authority than navigation or footer links
The anchor text of internal links is a ranking signal — vary it naturally between branded, topical, and generic anchors
⚠️ Common Mistakes
Linking to everything from everything — dilutes link equity and makes navigation confusing
Using 'click here' as anchor text — passes no topical relevance signal to Google
Only linking to the homepage — each page should distribute equity to the pages you want to rank
Not building category hub pages — without hub pages, internal equity doesn't accumulate anywhere meaningful
❓ FAQ
  • How many internal links per page is optimal?
    For most pages, 5–15 contextual internal links is appropriate. Navigation and footer links are separate. The key metric is relevance — every link should make sense in context and help the reader. Beyond 20–30 links, Google may dilute the PageRank passed by each.
  • Do internal links affect SEO rankings?
    Yes — internal links are a confirmed ranking factor. They distribute PageRank (link authority) through your site, signal page importance to Google, and help Google understand your site's content hierarchy. Pages with more high-quality internal links pointing to them typically rank better for target keywords.
  • What's the fastest internal linking win?
    Find your highest-traffic pages (in Google Analytics or Search Console) and add links from them to your most important pages that don't yet rank well. This transfers existing authority to pages that need it, often with measurable ranking improvements in 4–8 weeks.
  • Should internal links open in a new tab?
    No — internal links should open in the same tab. Opening internal links in new tabs frustrates users who expect same-tab navigation and interferes with the natural flow of exploration. Reserve 'open in new tab' only for external links.
