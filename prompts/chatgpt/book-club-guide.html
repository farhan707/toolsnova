📋 Prompt
You are an experienced book club facilitator and literature teacher who has run 100+ sessions.

Book: [title and author]
Group type: [casual friends/academic/workplace/mixed ages]
Meeting length: [60/90/120 minutes]
Group size: [number of people]

Task:
1. CONTEXT (2-3 paragraphs): Author background, when/why written, how received, why it matters now
2. KEY THEMES (3-4): Name, explanation, evidence from book, real-world connection
3. DISCUSSION QUESTIONS (15-20):
Tier 1 (Opening — easy): 4 questions
Tier 2 (Analytical — examine the text): 6 questions
Tier 3 (Thematic — bigger ideas): 5 questions
Tier 4 (Personal — connect to own life): 3 questions
4. CHARACTER SPOTLIGHT: 2-3 complex characters with analysis questions
5. CONTROVERSIAL MOMENTS: 2-3 passages or decisions that will generate debate
6. ACTIVITIES: Opening (5 min), mid-meeting (10 min)
7. FACILITATOR TIPS: Keep discussion flowing, handle dominant voices, draw out quiet members
📄 Example output
BOOK CLUB GUIDE: Educated by Tara Westover

CONTEXT:
Tara Westover grew up in a survivalist family in rural Idaho with no formal education until she taught herself to pass the ACT at 17 and earned a PhD from Cambridge. Educated (2018) is her memoir about that journey — and about memory, family loyalty, and self-invention.

Westover includes footnotes noting where family members remember events differently. The book doesn't resolve these contradictions — it asks readers to sit with them.

THEMES:
1. EDUCATION AS IDENTITY: Education provides not just knowledge but a framework for questioning the framework she grew up with. Is education liberation or also a form of indoctrination?
2. MEMORY AND TRUTH: Multiple contradictory accounts of the same events. What is truth in a family that constructs its own reality?

TIER 1 — OPENING:
1. What was your first reaction to Tara's childhood?
2. Which moment stayed with you most?
3. How do you feel about Tara's parents by the end?
4. If you could ask Tara one question, what would it be?

TIER 3 — THEMATIC:
1. Westover suggests education changed how she saw her past. Do you think education changes our memories or just our interpretation of them?
2. When should loyalty to family end? Where do you draw that line?

FACILITATOR TIPS:
Open with Tier 1 questions directed to specific people — 'Sarah, what was your first reaction?' not 'what does everyone think?'
For trauma sections: acknowledge the material is heavy, normalise varied responses, don't push for personal sharing.

💡 Pro Tips
Best discussion questions don't have a right answer — if there's a clearly correct response, it's a quiz, not a discussion
Open with personal response to get everyone talking before analytical questions
Prepare 20 questions but plan to use 8 — best discussions go deep on a few rather than skim many
Reading a short passage aloud during the meeting refreshes everyone's memory and typically increases engagement
⚠️ Common Mistakes
Questions that can be answered yes/no — always add 'and why?' or 'what made you feel that way?'
Too many questions — 20 in 90 minutes allows only 4 minutes each; choose 8 and let discussion breathe
Facilitator talking too much — your job is to hold space, not to lecture
No opening activity — jumping straight to analysis before people settle produces stilted discussion
❓ FAQ
  • Ideal meeting length?
    90 minutes is the sweet spot. 60 works for shorter books. 2+ hours only works for groups who know each other well.
  • People who haven't finished?
    10-minute summary at the start. 'Safe until page X' rule for spoilers. Non-finishers often ask the most useful clarifying questions.
  • Best model?
    Claude produces more analytically sophisticated thematic analysis and writes questions with genuine intellectual tension. Gemini is useful for author context and historical background.
  • How to handle a book people hate?
    Lean into it — 'What specifically didn't work?' produces better discussion than 'did you like it?'. Controversial books often generate the best discussion.
